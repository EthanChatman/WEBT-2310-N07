Everyday Life

More free time in evenings (i.e., less forced confinement) so you have more opportunities to hang out, progress confidants, or do other activities. New mini-games and activities in Kichijoji (darts, billiards, meditation temple) which provide combat-stat or social-stat benefits (e.g., boosting Baton Pass level, boosting Tech damage). An “Assist” mechanic: suggestions for which confidant to visit, which stat to improve — helpful especially for new players.

Quality of Life

Improved opening movie, new main menu screen, updated dialogue font, additional weather effects, etc. Better balance: Persona and enemy stats adjusted; battles feel more fluid and faster. The base game plus all these enhancements mean that P5R is the “definitive” version of P5.

Why These Matter

If you played the original P5, many of the changes in P5R make returning to the game feel different — you’ll explore new areas, experience modified boss fights, and gain more meaningful rewards for side-content. The grappling hook + Palace modifications help mitigate some of the tedium of dungeon exploration in the original by introducing more traversal/secret-finding. The new combat mechanics (Showtime attacks, ammo restock, third Persona forms) give more depth to late-game progression, meaning there’s a stronger “new game plus” or “end-game” experience. The extra everyday life options (Kichijoji, darts/billiards, temple meditation) make the non-dungeon portions more varied and meaningful rather than just filler. UI and quality-of-life enhancements reduce friction (less waiting, better balance, more meaningful choices) and thus improve the overall experience.
